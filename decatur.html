Case Study (Documentation & Governance)

City of Decatur, GA

Architecting a City's Preservation Standards

10+ Years

of Scattered Policy Unified

The Problem

The City of Decatur's Historic Preservation Commission (HPC) faced a systemic governance crisis. Their rules for homeowners and developers were scattered across aging legal reports, conflicting city ordinances, and outdated manuals—with no single source of truth. This fragmentation led to confusion, project delays, and friction in the approval process.

The Solution & My Work

Working with a cross-functional team, I developed the architectural taxonomy system and process documentation for a 160-page governance manual—consolidating over a decade of fragmented preservation policies into the city's first centralized source of truth for design standards.

The core challenge wasn't just collecting information—it was transforming dense, subjective legal language and undocumented institutional knowledge into clear, enforceable standards that three distinct audiences (homeowners, developers, and the Commission) could actually use.

Information Architecture and Taxonomy

The project began with auditing scattered documentation—legal reports from the 1990s, individual property surveys, architectural studies, and commission meeting minutes. I was responsible for defining the methodology to consolidate this disparate data into a logical structure.

The Challenge

Many buildings didn't fit neatly into a single category. A 1920s Craftsman cottage in one neighborhood might have different preservation requirements than the same style in another district. I had to create a classification system that accounted for both architectural type (form/function) and architectural style (aesthetic period), then map these across 14 distinct historic neighborhoods.

The result was a comprehensive taxonomy of 18 building types and 10 architectural styles, cross-referenced by neighborhood character. This became the backbone of the entire 160-page manual—ensuring every rule could be traced back to a specific, findable classification.

Artifact: Architectural Taxonomy & Classification System

Process Definition and Standardization

Beyond taxonomy, the city needed a clear workflow. The approval process involved two distinct permit types (Certificate of Appropriateness and Certificate of Exemption), multiple decision points, and an appeals mechanism—but none of this was documented in a way applicants could follow.

The Challenge

The existing process lived in the heads of long-tenured staff and commission members. New applicants had no way to know: Do I need a permit? Which type? What happens if I'm denied? How long will this take? The legal language in city ordinances answered these questions—but only if you could parse Section 58-3 of the municipal code.

I translated the complex legal workflow into a visual decision tree, mapping out every possible path from initial inquiry through final approval. This included defining trigger questions (Is the property in a historic district? Does the work affect exterior features?), documenting all three possible outcomes (approval, conditional approval, denial), and clarifying the appeals process with specific timelines.

The Payoff

The governance manual became the single source of truth. Homeowners could self-serve permit questions. Developers could plan with confidence. Staff could enforce consistently. What was fragmented became findable.
