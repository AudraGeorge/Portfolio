PORTFOLIO PIECE 2: DELOITTE CASE STUDY

Documentation & Knowledge Infrastructure for Global Creative Operations

Client: Deloitte (Global Consulting Firm) Role: Creative Operations Specialist Timeline: 2018 – August 2022 Tools: Microsoft SharePoint, Google Drive, Adobe Workfront, Confluence

PROJECT OVERVIEW

Background

Deloitte's creative operations team produces marketing collateral, presentations, brand materials, and campaign assets for a global consulting practice. Initially supporting U.S. and Canada operations with approximately 20 team members, the department expanded to serve international markets across EMEA, APAC, and Latin America, growing to 40+ members spanning multiple time zones.

Challenge

As the team scaled globally, operational knowledge remained largely undocumented and concentrated in the heads of a few long-tenured team members. New hires faced steep learning curves, cross-time-zone collaboration suffered from information gaps, and inconsistent processes created inefficiencies.

Key Pain Points:
  • 80% of operational knowledge existed only verbally (not written down anywhere)
  • No centralized documentation repository (information scattered across emails, Slack threads, individual hard drives)
  • New team members took 6-8 weeks to onboard due to lack of structured training materials
  • Heavy reliance on synchronous communication (constant Slack messages asking "how do I...?" or "where is...?")
  • Process inconsistencies across regions (U.S. team operated differently than EMEA team)
  • Knowledge loss risk when team members left (no documentation of their workflows)

    • Objective

    Build a documentation and knowledge-sharing infrastructure that would:

  • Capture institutional knowledge in accessible, searchable formats
  • Enable asynchronous collaboration across time zones
  • Reduce onboarding time for new team members
  • Standardize processes globally while accommodating regional variations
  • Create self-service systems to reduce dependency on asking colleagues for information

    • ASSESSMENT & DISCOVERY

    Discovery Process

    Phase 1: Knowledge Audit (Weeks 1-4)

    Conducted interviews and observation to map existing operational knowledge:

    Interviews with 15 team members across roles:
  • What processes do you perform regularly?
  • Where do you go when you need information?
  • What do new team members struggle to learn?
  • What questions do you get asked repeatedly?
    • Shadow sessions with 6 team members:
  • Observed daily workflows to identify undocumented steps
  • Noted where team members hit roadblocks or needed to ask for help
  • Cataloged tools, templates, and resources in use
    • Survey of all 40+ team members:
  • Rated confidence in finding information independently (1-10 scale)
  • Identified top 10 most confusing or unclear processes
  • Suggested topics for documentation
    • Phase 2: Gap Analysis (Week 5)

    Analyzed findings to identify highest-impact documentation needs:

    Critical Gaps Identified:
  • Project Intake & Workflow (mentioned by 87% of respondents)
  • Adobe Experience Manager (AEM) asset management (mentioned by 73%)
  • Legal and brand review processes (mentioned by 68%)
  • Vendor onboarding and management (mentioned by 54%)
  • Regional-specific requirements (mentioned by 61% of international team)

    • Key Findings

    1. Institutional Knowledge Trapped in People
  • Finding: 80% of operational processes existed only as verbal knowledge
  • Examples:

    • - "Ask Sarah about legal review routing"

    - "John knows how to expedite vendor payments"

    - "The EMEA team has a different intake form but I don't know where it is"

  • Impact: Bottlenecks when key people unavailable; knowledge lost when employees departed
    • 2. No Single Source of Truth
  • Finding: Information scattered across 7+ platforms with no central hub
  • Locations of "documentation" (such as it was):

    • - Slack threads (unsearchable after 90 days on free plan)

    - Individual email inboxes

    - Personal Google Drive folders (access lost when person left)

    - Random Word docs on network drives (no version control)

    - Sticky notes and handwritten notes

    - Tribal knowledge ("everyone just knows this")

  • Impact: Wasted time hunting for information; conflicting instructions; duplicated effort
    • 3. Onboarding Was Trial by Fire
  • Finding: New team members took 6-8 weeks to become productive
  • Onboarding "process":

    • - Week 1: IT setup, vague intro to tools

    - Weeks 2-6: Shadow teammates, ask lots of questions, piece together workflows

    - Weeks 6-8: Still asking basic questions; frustration on both sides

  • Impact: Slow ramp-up; high burden on existing team to answer repetitive questions; inconsistent training quality
    • 4. Time Zone Challenges
  • Finding: Asynchronous work nearly impossible due to lack of documentation
  • Scenario: APAC team member needs to complete a task but:

    • - Process not documented

    - U.S. team (who knows the process) is asleep

    - Work blocked for 12+ hours waiting for response

  • Impact: Delays, missed deadlines, frustration across global team
    • 5. Process Drift Across Regions
  • Finding: Teams in different regions developed their own workflows with no standardization
  • Example: Project intake process varied by region:

    • - U.S.: Submitted via Workfront

    - EMEA: Submitted via email to team lead

    - APAC: Submitted via Google Form

    - No central tracking; projects fell through cracks

  • Impact: Inconsistent service quality; leadership lacked visibility into global workload

    • SOLUTION DESIGN

    Centralized Knowledge Repository

    Created a structured documentation hub in SharePoint (chosen for integration with Microsoft ecosystem and global accessibility).

    Repository Structure:

    📁 Creative Operations Knowledge Base/

    ├── 📁 01_SOPs & Process Documentation/
    

    │   ├── Intake & Project Management
    

    │   ├── Asset Management & DAM
    

    │   ├── Legal & Brand Compliance
    

    │   ├── Vendor & Procurement
    

    │   └── Regional-Specific Processes
    

    ├── 📁 02_Training & Onboarding/
    

    │   ├── New Team Member Onboarding
    

    │   ├── Tool Training (AEM, Workfront, etc.)
    

    │   └── Process Training
    

    ├── 📁 03_Templates & Forms/
    

    │   ├── Project Intake Templates
    

    │   ├── Asset Request Forms
    

    │   └── Reporting Templates
    

    ├── 📁 04_Brand & Style Guidelines/
    

    │   ├── Global Brand Standards
    

    │   ├── Regional Brand Variations
    

    │   └── Legal & Compliance Guidelines
    

    └── 📁 05_Tool Documentation/
    

        ├── Adobe Experience Manager
    

        ├── Adobe Workfront
    

        └── Microsoft SharePoint
    Design Principles:
  • Function-based organization: Organized by what users need to accomplish, not by team or role
  • Shallow hierarchy: Maximum 3 levels deep to prevent "click fatigue"
  • Predictable structure: Similar subfolders across categories for easy navigation
  • Scalable: Accommodates new regions and tools without restructuring

    • Comprehensive SOP Development

    Created 50+ standard operating procedures covering critical workflows.

    SOP Template Format:

    Every SOP followed a consistent structure:

  • Purpose & Scope

    • - What this process accomplishes

    - When to use this SOP

    - Who is responsible

  • Prerequisites

    • - Required tools/access

    - Prior steps that must be completed

  • Step-by-Step Instructions

    • - Numbered steps with screenshots

    - Decision points clearly marked

    - "If/Then" scenarios addressed

  • Troubleshooting

    • - Common issues and solutions

    - Escalation path if problems persist

  • Related Documentation

    • - Links to related SOPs

    - Relevant templates or forms

  • Contacts & Support

    • - Who to ask for help

    - Slack channel for questions

    Example SOPs Created:
  • Project Intake Workflow (U.S., EMEA, APAC regional variations)
  • Legal Review Routing Process
  • AEM Asset Upload Standards
  • Vendor Onboarding Process
  • Brand Approval Workflow
  • Timeline Estimation Guidelines

    Templates Included:
  • Project Request Forms (intake)
  • Asset Request Forms
  • Creative Briefs
  • Legal Review Checklists
  • Vendor Onboarding Packets

    • Onboarding Curriculum

    Developed structured 4-week onboarding program for new team members.

    Onboarding Structure:

    Week 1: Orientation & Access
  • IT setup and tool access
  • Introduction to knowledge base navigation
  • Review of team structure and roles
  • Assign onboarding buddy
    • Week 2: Core Processes
  • Training on project intake workflow
  • Asset management and DAM basics
  • Introduction to legal and brand review processes
  • Shadow experienced team member on 2-3 projects
    • Week 3: Tool Training
  • Adobe Experience Manager (AEM) hands-on training
  • Adobe Workfront project management training
  • SharePoint and knowledge base deep dive
  • Practice projects with feedback
    • Week 4: Independent Work with Support
  • Manage 2-3 small projects independently
  • Weekly check-ins with manager
  • Access to onboarding buddy for questions
  • End-of-month readiness assessment
    • Onboarding Materials Created:
  • Week-by-week checklists
  • Training videos (screen recordings with voiceover)
  • Quick reference guides for each tool
  • Self-assessment quizzes
  • Onboarding completion survey
    • Results:
  • Onboarding time reduced from 6-8 weeks to 3-4 weeks
  • New team members reported 85% confidence in core processes by end of Week 4
  • Reduced burden on existing team (fewer repetitive questions)

    • Governance Framework

    Established standards for creating, maintaining, and accessing documentation.

    Key Governance Elements: 1. Documentation Standards
  • All SOPs must include: purpose, prerequisites, step-by-step instructions, troubleshooting, contacts
  • Consistent formatting and structure
  • Required screenshots and visual aids
  • Version control via file naming
    • 2. Review & Update Cycles
  • SOPs reviewed quarterly
  • Training materials reviewed semi-annually
  • Templates reviewed annually
  • Ad-hoc updates triggered by process changes or tool updates
    • 3. Ownership & Accountability
  • Creative Operations Specialist: Overall framework ownership
  • Team Leads: Subject matter expertise for their functional areas
  • All team members: Flagging outdated or unclear documentation
    • 4. Quality Assurance
  • Pre-publication checklist for all new documentation
  • Peer review required before publishing SOPs
  • Quarterly audit of documentation completeness
    • 5. Access Controls
  • All team: Read access to SOPs and training materials
  • Team leads: Edit access to documentation in their functional area
  • Creative Ops Specialist: Full control across all documentation

    • IMPLEMENTATION & OUTCOMES

    Implementation Approach

    Phase 1: Pilot Documentation (Weeks 6-10)
  • Created 10 highest-priority SOPs based on discovery findings
  • Built onboarding curriculum and templates
  • Tested with small group (5 team members) for feedback
  • Refined based on usability testing
    • Phase 2: Knowledge Base Rollout (Weeks 11-14)
  • Launched SharePoint repository with pilot content
  • Conducted live training sessions (3 sessions across time zones)
  • Created "Knowledge Base Champions" in each region for peer support
  • Distributed quick reference guide for navigating repository
    • Phase 3: Content Expansion (Weeks 15-26)
  • Systematically created remaining 40+ SOPs
  • Built out template library
  • Recorded training videos for complex tools
  • Organized brand and legal documentation
    • Phase 4: Onboarding Curriculum Launch (Week 27)
  • Piloted 4-week onboarding program with 2 new hires
  • Gathered feedback and refined materials
  • Rolled out globally as standard onboarding approach
    • Phase 5: Ongoing Maintenance (Weeks 28+)
  • Established quarterly review schedule
  • Monitored usage analytics to identify gaps
  • Continuously refined based on team feedback

  • Waits for response (could be hours if asker is in different time zone)
  • Receives verbal explanation or forwarded email with partial instructions
  • Piece together process from fragmented information
    • After:

  • Finds SOP: "Legal Review Routing Process"

  • Completes task independently in 15 minutes
  • No need to ask colleagues; no waiting for response

    • Sample SOP Structure

    SOP: Legal Review Routing for Creative Assets 1. Purpose & Scope
  • This SOP outlines the process for routing creative assets through Deloitte's legal review and approval workflow before public release.
  • Use this process for: marketing materials, client-facing presentations, social media posts, advertisements.
    • 2. Prerequisites
  • Asset must be uploaded to Adobe Experience Manager (AEM)
  • Creative brief and project details must be logged in Workfront
  • Requestor must have AEM and Workfront access
    • 3. Step-by-Step Instructions Step 1: Initiate Legal Review Request in Workfront
  • Open the project in Workfront
  • Click "Tasks" tab
  • Select "Request Legal Review" task
  • Fill in required fields:

    • - Asset name and AEM link

    - Intended usage (web, print, social, etc.)

    - Target publish date

    - Urgency level

  • Click "Submit"

    • [Screenshot of Workfront task form]

    Step 2: Tag Asset in AEM for Legal Review
  • Navigate to asset in AEM
  • Update metadata field: "Review Status" → "Legal Review Pending"
  • Add tag: "LegalReview_[YYYY-MM]"
  • Save changes

    • [Screenshot of AEM metadata panel]

    Step 3: Notify Legal Team
  • Send email to legal-review@deloitte.com
  • Subject: "Legal Review Request: [Asset Name]"
  • Include: Workfront task link, AEM asset link, target publish date
  • CC your manager

    • [Screenshot of email template]

    Step 4: Track Review Status
  • Monitor Workfront task for updates
  • Legal team SLA: 5 business days standard, 2 business days expedited
  • If no response within SLA, escalate to [Manager Name]
    • Step 5: Address Legal Feedback
  • Legal team will comment directly in Workfront task
  • Make requested revisions to asset
  • Upload revised version to AEM with version number incremented (v2, v3, etc.)
  • Reply in Workfront task when revisions complete
    • Step 6: Final Approval & Asset Release
  • Once legal approves, update AEM metadata: "Review Status" → "Approved"
  • Mark Workfront task as complete
  • Asset is now cleared for public release
    • 4. Troubleshooting
    IssueSolution
    Legal team hasn't responded within SLACheck spam folder for emails; if still no response, escalate to [Manager]
    Asset requires expedited reviewMark "Urgency: High" in Workfront; follow up with phone call to legal team
    Unclear legal feedbackSchedule 15-min call with legal reviewer to clarify; document outcome in Workfront

    • CONCLUSION

    Building a comprehensive documentation and knowledge-sharing infrastructure enabled Deloitte's creative operations team to scale globally while maintaining operational efficiency and service quality. By capturing institutional knowledge, standardizing processes, and creating self-service systems, the team reduced onboarding time, minimized dependency on synchronous communication, and empowered team members across all time zones to work independently.

    Key Success Factors:
  • User-centered discovery: Understanding actual pain points before building solutions
  • Comprehensive governance: Ensuring documentation stayed current and useful over time
  • Structured onboarding: Transforming chaotic trial-by-fire into systematic training
  • Cross-functional collaboration: Involving team leads as subject matter experts ensured accuracy and adoption

The knowledge infrastructure continues to support global creative operations, enabling asynchronous collaboration and sustainable growth.
